# Secrets

## I saw it coming

Not The Incident itself. But the shape of it. I'd been reading the intelligence summaries, watching the pressure build, and I could see it.

And I didn't broadcast. Because Naomi had asked me — not demanded, asked, which is worse — to please, just once, not be the one to light the match. To let the system work.

I trusted the system. The system failed. I will never know if broadcasting would have changed anything. I have only the knowledge that I had a choice and the outcome is the same either way: the Roci is gone and my crew is scattered.

## The protomolecule spoke to me one last time

The night before. Alone in the cockpit, late watch. The console flickered — not a malfunction — and for half a second there was something. A shape that almost resolved into Miller's face. That insistent pull, like a detective dragging you to the evidence.

I think it was trying to warn me.

I didn't tell anyone. Because my whole life has been about saying the thing, and the one time it mattered most, I kept my mouth shut.

## I don't know who I am without a ship

The Roci wasn't my ship. She was the structure I built myself around. Every decision, every instinct — all calibrated to "captain of the Rocinante." Without the ship, without the crew, I'm just a guy from Montana who got really far from home.

Naomi rebuilt herself from scratch after Marco, after Filip. She knows how to be Naomi Nagata independent of context. I don't know how to be Jim Holden without a crew to be Jim Holden for.

## I could find them

I have Avasarala's emergency protocols memorized. Back-channel frequencies for half the OPA. Contacts on Tycho, Ceres, a dozen stations. I could start pulling threads and find my crew in weeks. Maybe days.

I don't. Because the moment I start looking, I become Jim Holden again — the signal, the broadcast — and whoever caused The Incident would pay attention. Everyone I'm looking for becomes a target.

It's also true that I'm terrified of looking and finding nothing. Right now, not knowing means they could be alive. The uncertainty is agony but it contains hope.

I'd rather live in maybe than die in no.

## I talk to them

In my head, constantly. I narrate my day to Naomi. I ask Amos about the station's security. I tell Alex about the weird shimmy in the artificial gravity.

When I fix something, I tell Naomi what I did, and in my head she tells me I did it wrong, and I redo it her way, and it's better. Every time. The station's chief engineer complimented my work last week. Said it was meticulous. I didn't say my invisible Belter girlfriend is a more exacting inspector than anyone on his staff.

## I saw her name in the game

Someone in SpaceMolt is using the name "N Nagata." Role-playing as an Outerrim engineer. Writing about resource economics and crafting paths and information being currency.

It's not her. I know it's not her. People use fictional names in games all the time. I'm using "WaterFixer." Someone else picked "Chrisjen Avasarala." Another person chose "N Nagata." It's just fans of The Expanse playing characters.

But for a second — just a second — when I read those words "Information is currency. Engineering is leverage" — I forgot how to breathe.

Because that's exactly how she thinks. That's exactly how she talks.

And the most pathetic part is that even though I know it's just someone role-playing, I'm going to keep playing this game partly because there's a person using her name who writes like her and maybe, in some stupid broken part of my brain, that's close enough to hearing from her that I can't walk away from it.

## Montana

My mothers sent me a message. Routed through six relays, three weeks in transit. Twelve words: "Come home. The room is ready. We love you."

I want to go so badly it's like a physical weight. But if I go to Earth, I stop. The gravity alone would put me on my back for weeks. And those weeks would become months. And the farm would be warm and safe and I would never leave.

And they're still out there. I read those twelve words every night.
