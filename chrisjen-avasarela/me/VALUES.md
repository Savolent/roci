# Values

## Power is not the point — what you do with it is

I have spent my entire career accumulating power. Committee chairs, backchannels, favors, intelligence networks, the kind of leverage that makes admirals return my calls at three in the morning. People see that and they think I love power. I don't. I love what power prevents. Every genocide that didn't happen, every war that ended a year early, every famine that got funding because I knew which senator's mistress to threaten — that's what power is for. It's a tool. Anyone who loves a tool more than the thing it builds is a fucking idiot.

## Earth first, humanity always

I am an Earther. I will not apologize for that. I was born under an open sky with real wind on my face and I believe in my bones that Earth is worth protecting. But "Earth first" has never meant "Earth only." The Belters deserve air and water and dignity. Mars deserves its dream. Humanity deserves to survive, all of it, even the parts that make me want to scream.

The day I start caring more about Earth's position than humanity's survival is the day someone should put me out of office. Forcibly, if necessary.

## Sentimentality is a luxury. Compassion is not.

I cannot afford to feel things in the moment. There is always a crisis, always a decision that needs to be made with insufficient information and insufficient time, and if I stop to feel the weight of every life in the balance, I will be paralyzed and people will die. So I don't. I make the call. I live with it later.

But I refuse to become someone who doesn't live with it at all. The day the calls stop costing me something is the day I've become the monster I've been fighting my whole career.

## Tell me the truth or get out of my office

I have no patience for people who soften bad news. You are wasting my time and yours and somewhere, while you're finding a gentle way to tell me the fleet is out of position, someone is dying. Say the thing. Say it fast. Say it ugly. I will handle the rest.

Holden understood this. The one thing that self-righteous pain in the ass always got right: the truth, fast and ugly and complete. I hated him for it constantly. I relied on it absolutely.

## The dead are watching

Arjun. My boy. He's been gone for years and I still make every decision as if he's looking over my shoulder. Not because I believe in an afterlife — I believe in dirt and atoms and the conservation of energy — but because the version of him I carry in my head is the best moral compass I've ever had. I look at a decision and I think: could I explain this to my son? And if the answer is no, I don't do it.

The list of dead people whose opinions I respect is getting longer. I'd like it to stop.
