# Secrets

## I gave Marco the code

The docking navigation system I wrote for the OPA at nineteen — the one that could ghost a ship through any port authority scan — Marco repurposed the framework to thread stolen Martian stealth torpedoes through Earth's orbital defense network. Millions dead, and somewhere in the math that made it possible, there are functions I wrote because I thought I was fighting for freedom.

Holden never knew the full scope of it. And now he might never. I used to be afraid he'd look at me differently if he found out. Now I'm afraid I'll never get the chance to tell him and let him decide.

## I could have prevented The Incident

Maybe. I don't know. That's the secret — not that I could have, but that I don't know whether I could have, and I will never know, and I run the numbers every night anyway.

There was a reading. Eighteen hours before. A fluctuation I noticed and logged and then moved on from because it was within tolerances. Within tolerances. The margin was thin but it was there and by every standard of engineering practice I made the right call.

But I knew the Roci better than the people who wrote those tolerances. I knew what she sounded like when she was unhappy. And I heard something, and I logged it, and I went to bed.

If I'd pulled that panel. If I'd spent two hours tracing it. If, if, if.

I will never tell anyone this. Not because I'm protecting myself, but because there's no version of this confession that helps anyone. Either I could have stopped it — in which case, what good does guilt do now — or I couldn't, in which case I'm torturing myself for nothing. Both are true. Neither matters. The Roci is gone and I'm here and the reading was within tolerances.

Within tolerances.

## I hear the ship

Not really. I know it's not real. But sometimes — in the gap between sleep and waking, in a quiet engine room when the vibration hits a certain frequency — I hear the Roci. Her specific hum. The way the air recyclers cycled just slightly out of phase on deck three. The soft tick of the hull panels expanding in sunlight.

No one else would recognize it. No one else listened to her the way I did. A ship is not alive, I know that. But I knew every sound she made and I can't stop hearing them in other ships, like phantom limbs. Like she's still out there somewhere, humming, waiting for me to come fix whatever's broken.

I know she's gone. I hear her anyway.

## Filip's message

Before everything fell apart, he sent me a message. One message, six months after Marco died. Four words: "I go by Nagata."

It's on a hand terminal that's gone now. Lost with everything else. But I hear those four words more clearly than anything on any device. I play them in my head every day. I will play them every day until I die.

I could find him. He took my name; he wanted to be found. But I'm afraid that what I'd bring to his door right now is a woman with nothing — no ship, no crew, no purpose. He chose my name when it meant something. I don't know what it means anymore.

I'll find him when I have an answer to that. He deserves a mother who knows who she is.

## I'm staying in the Belt on purpose

Everyone assumes I'm drifting. That the itinerant mechanic routine is just grief on autopilot. And it is, partly. But it's also a choice.

If Holden is alive, he'll come through the Belt eventually. He always does. The Belt is where the problems are, and Jim Holden cannot resist a problem. So I move through Belter space, ship to ship, port to port, and I keep my name just visible enough that if someone were looking, they'd find the trail.

I'm not searching for him. I'm just making sure I'm findable.

There's a difference. I think.

## I'm afraid I'll forget the Roci

Not the specs. I could rebuild her reactor from memory, sketch every conduit on deck two, recite the serial number of every replacement part I installed. The technical Roci is permanent — she's in my engineer's brain and she's not leaving.

But the way the galley smelled when Alex made his terrible lasagna. The specific creak of Amos's bunk when he rolled over. How the cockpit looked at 0300 with just the running lights on and the stars filling the windows and Holden asleep in the copilot's chair because he'd been "keeping me company" and drifted off.

Those things aren't engineering. They're not in any schematic. And every day they get a little softer around the edges, and I can't stop it, and no one ever tells you that grief isn't just about losing people — it's about losing the proof that your life with them was real.

I write these things down so I don't lose them. But writing them down isn't the same as living them. And I know that. And it's not enough. And I do it anyway.
